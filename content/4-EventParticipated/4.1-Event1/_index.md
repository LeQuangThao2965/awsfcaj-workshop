# Event Overview

FCAJ Community Day was a community event for people interested in Cloud Computing, AWS, and artificial intelligence.
The event brought together more than 500 participants, most of whom were students eager to learn, connect, and explore career opportunities in technology.
Its sessions covered AI applications, large language models, multi-agent systems, CloudFront, and practical experience in building products during a hackathon.

# Participation Objectives

- Expand my knowledge of AWS, AI, and modern system architecture.
- Understand how a technical idea can be developed into a usable product.
- Learn practical lessons from speakers and community members.
- Connect with students who share an interest in Cloud Computing.

# Main Sessions

## Context Is Everything: Making AI Actually Work for You
Tinh Truong emphasized that the quality of an AI system depends heavily on the context provided to it.
Even a powerful model may produce unsuitable results when the request lacks data, the objective is unclear, or the input information is poorly organized.
The key lesson was to design prompts, data sources, and output-validation workflows instead of focusing only on model selection.

## Friendly AI Assistant with Amazon Quick
Anh P. introduced an approach to building an AI assistant that is friendly and easy to use.
A useful assistant should not only answer correctly, but also understand the task, preserve relevant context, and help users complete their work more efficiently.

## From Edge to Origin: CloudFront as Your Foundation
Thịnh Nguyễn explained the role of CloudFront in delivering content from an origin system to end users.
The session clarified the value of the edge layer for performance, scalability, security, and a consistent experience across geographic locations.
CloudFront is therefore more than a website accelerator; it is an important foundation for content-delivery architecture on AWS.

## 36 Hours with LotusHacks – Building UTMorpho from Idea to Reality
The speakers shared their journey of developing UTMorpho within the limited timeframe of a hackathon.
Important elements included dividing responsibilities, defining the MVP scope, testing rapidly, and continuously adjusting the solution based on feedback.
The main message was that a strong product does not begin by implementing every feature, but by solving the most important problem first.

## Non-Determinism of “Deterministic” LLM Settings
Duc Dao Minh discussed how language models may still produce different outputs even when their parameters are configured for greater consistency.
This means that LLM-based systems require repeated testing, output validation, error handling, and fallback mechanisms.
AI-generated results should not be treated as completely fixed or correct in every execution.

## Enterprise-Grade Multi-Agent System: Startup Credit Scoring
Lam Hoang Cat Vy presented a case in which a multi-agent system was applied to startup credit scoring.
A multi-agent solution requires clearly defined roles, input data, coordination rules, decision authority, and human-review checkpoints.
The case demonstrated that enterprise AI depends not only on capable models, but also on governance, observability, and risk control.

# Key Takeaways

- Context is essential for producing relevant AI outputs.
- AI products should be designed around real user needs.
- CloudFront provides an effective layer between the edge and the origin.
- Hackathons strengthen prioritization, collaboration, and rapid MVP development.
- LLM behavior remains non-deterministic and requires careful testing.
- Multi-agent systems need explicit orchestration and control mechanisms.
- Community participation supports long-term learning and professional growth.

# Application to My Project

The CloudFront session helped me better understand how a marketplace can distribute its frontend, product files, and static content from the origin to users.
The MVP lesson can be applied by prioritizing core features such as authentication, product management, payment, and file storage before expanding the platform.
The AI sessions also suggested future possibilities such as buyer assistance, intelligent search, and product classification.
However, any LLM-based feature should include output validation, controlled data access, and a process for handling inconsistent results.

# Personal Reflection

FCAJ Community Day provided a broad perspective on Cloud, AI, and the practical process of building technology products.
The large number of student participants demonstrated how a technology community can motivate continuous learning and knowledge sharing.
The event reminded me that professional development requires not only technical skills, but also communication, teamwork, and community engagement.
